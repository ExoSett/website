Fire

How an ExoSett building could contain a localised fire, protect escape routes and recover by replacing damaged modules.

A couple of friends were granted social housing in our ExoSett building. This was their very first time living away from their parents, and they decided to have a barbecue in the flat using a disposable charcoal barbecue perched on unopened packing boxes. This did not go so well. They managed to set fire to the barbecue, their packing boxes and the contents of their new flat.

The first we knew of it was the building fire alarm sounding. It took a little while to realise it was not a drill, but once we knew it was the real thing, we grabbed warm clothes, left our flat and joined the brisk walk to the nearest stairs.

A little later, in the car park around the building, officers of the fire brigade explained that the fire was out and appeared not to have spread beyond the dwelling where it started. It would still take some time to make sure the building was entirely safe.

Later still, in the local pub, officers came by to let residents know that there was some smoke damage in the hallways, mostly a lingering smell, but the only accommodation affected directly by the fire was the dwelling where it had started.

In this building, the fire-safety measures had contained the fire within the flat where it began. There was a significant smell of smoke in the hallways immediately afterwards, but the service frame was opened and aired, and the hallway carpets were replaced quite quickly. There was a slight smoke smell in our flat, but that soon faded.

The fire-damaged modules were removed. The exposed frame cells and adjacent modules were inspected and cleaned before replacement modules were installed. I think the young residents have gone back to live with their parents for now, and new people have since moved in.

What this story illustrates

Fire safety in an ExoSett building must be addressed both at the level of the individual module and at the level of the overall system.

Accommodation modules can be designed and certified to applicable fire-safety standards, providing suitable compartmentation for occupants and contents. Module specifications may address fire resistance, internal finishes, smoke detection and alarms, together with fire-suppression systems where these are required by the fire strategy or regulations, or otherwise chosen for the project.

At the system level, separation between modules, independent structural support and accessible service routing can help reduce the available paths for fire and smoke to spread. Escape routes through the service frame can be designed with suitable capacity, separation and protection, as well as alternative directions of travel where required.

Because the accommodation frame remains structurally independent of the modules, severe damage to an individual module need not imply loss of the primary building structure. Once the incident has been investigated, the frame and adjacent accommodation have been inspected, smoke contamination has been remediated and the building has been declared safe, damaged modules may be removed and replacement accommodation installed, subject to any necessary regulatory approval.

Together, these features may support an approach in which a localised incident can be contained and the building recovered without widespread demolition or long-term closure. They are not automatic properties of every ExoSett building: the fire strategy, module ratings, suppression provision, escape design and approvals would depend on the building’s use, height and location, and on the applicable regulations.
