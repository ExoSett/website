ExoSett component

Accommodation frame

The accommodation frame is the primary load-bearing structure that contains and independently supports accommodation modules.

It defines the structural geometry of an ExoSett building, transfers loads to the foundations and remains viable whether its cells are occupied or empty.

Definition

The structure that hosts the accommodation

An accommodation frame is the principal structural framework of an ExoSett building.

It contains a regular or otherwise defined arrangement of cells into which accommodation modules can be installed. The frame supports each module independently and transfers structural loads through its columns, beams, frame nodes, bracing and foundations.

The accommodation frame is distinct from the accommodation modules it hosts. The modules do not form an indispensable part of the primary load-bearing structure.

Purpose

Carry the building while allowing the accommodation to change

The principal purpose of the accommodation frame is to carry the structural loads of the building without depending on the continued presence of any particular accommodation module.

This allows modules to be installed, removed, replaced or relocated without requiring the modules around or beneath them to assume their structural role.

The frame therefore provides continuity. Accommodation may change over time while useful structural infrastructure remains in service.

Key characteristics

What defines an ExoSett accommodation frame

The frame is structurally viable when some or all of its cells are empty.

Each accommodation module is supported by the frame rather than by the accommodation module below it.

No individual accommodation module is structurally indispensable to neighbouring modules.

The frame provides defined structural locations for supporting, securing and handling installed modules.

Structural loads pass through identified frame components and interfaces towards the foundations or other supporting structure.

The frame must possess sufficient strength, stability and stiffness for its intended arrangement, including when modules do not contribute to lateral stiffness.

Geometry

A framework of cells and structural intersections

An accommodation frame is formed from columns, beams and frame nodes arranged to define a series of cells.

A cell is the space allocated to one accommodation module. Each installed module occupies one cell, although adjacent modules may be connected to create a larger combined space.

The frame geometry can be described using frame addresses. These identify continuing locations and roles within the structure even when the physical component occupying a location is replaced.

Structural behaviour

Defined paths for gravity and lateral loads

Gravity loads from accommodation modules, roofs and other supported elements are introduced into the frame at defined support points. They are then transferred through frame nodes and columns towards the foundations.

The frame must also resist lateral effects such as wind, accidental forces, racking and twisting. Depending on the implementation, this stiffness may be provided by rigid or semi-rigid connections, lashings, diagonal bracing, stabilising frames, façades or other engineered systems.

Accommodation modules may contribute to stiffness in some designs, but the frame must not depend upon an individual module remaining in place unless that dependency is deliberately engineered and compatible with the defining principles of the system.

Interfaces

Where the frame meets the rest of the building

The accommodation frame interfaces with accommodation modules at their support and restraint points.

It interfaces with the service frame through bridges, walkways, structural connections and the clearances required for access and module installation.

It interfaces with foundations below, roofs above and façades, bracing systems or weather protection around its perimeter.

It may also provide defined docking points for an installation frame or other equipment used to insert and remove modules.

Variants

A system role, not a single fixed design

Accommodation frames may vary in width, depth and height. Their cells may host standardised modules, project-specific modules or a mixture of module sizes.

Frames may be made from steel, timber, concrete or combinations of materials where those materials can satisfy the structural, durability, manufacturing and disassembly requirements of the project.

They may stand alone, be arranged in pairs around a shared service frame, be connected to other structures or be supported on foundations, platforms, barges or other engineered bases.

Their connection behaviour, bracing arrangement, module supports, fire protection, corrosion protection and installation methods may also differ between implementations.

Distinction

Not a stack of load-bearing modules

A conventional modular building may use the modules themselves as the primary structure, with upper modules supported by those below.

An ExoSett accommodation frame instead provides an external structural system that supports the modules individually. Removing a module does not remove the principal support for the accommodation above it.

This distinction is central to ExoSett. It is what allows the accommodation to change independently of the continuing structural framework.

Development status

A defined role under engineering development

The role of the accommodation frame within ExoSett is well defined, but its detailed engineering is still under development.

Current work includes frame geometry, load paths, connection behaviour, lateral stiffness, module support, installation access and the relationship between the accommodation frame and the service frame.

Every practical implementation would require project-specific structural design, analysis, testing and regulatory approval.
