ExoSett component

Service frame

The service frame provides access, circulation and routes for utilities to accommodation modules installed within an accommodation frame.

It separates the continuing operational infrastructure of the building from the removable accommodation modules, helping people, services and maintenance reach each module without making the modules themselves part of the shared access system.

Definition

The operational structure serving the accommodation

A service frame is a structure associated with an ExoSett accommodation frame that provides access to installed modules and supports the routes, equipment and connections required to operate them.

It may contain walkways, corridors, stairs, lifts, service risers, pipes, cables, ducts, access bridges and maintenance areas.

The service frame is distinct from both the accommodation frame and the accommodation modules. It performs a different and continuing role within the building.

Purpose

Keep access and services available while modules change

The principal purpose of the service frame is to provide the shared access and utility infrastructure needed by the accommodation modules.

When a module is installed, removed or replaced, the service frame can remain in place. It continues to provide circulation and service routes to the rest of the building while work is carried out at an individual cell.

This separation helps make the accommodation replaceable without repeatedly rebuilding stairs, corridors, lifts, risers and other shared infrastructure.

Key characteristics

What defines an ExoSett service frame

Normal operational access to installed accommodation modules is provided through the service frame.

Shared utility routes are carried outside the accommodation modules so they remain visible, accessible and capable of alteration.

Each module can be connected to and disconnected from the wider building through defined access and service interfaces.

The service frame remains useful when individual cells are empty or when modules are being installed, removed or replaced.

Its circulation, fire safety, accessibility, weather protection and structural design must suit the intended use of the building.

Access and circulation

The route to every occupied module

The service frame provides the shared routes by which occupants, visitors, staff and maintenance teams reach accommodation modules.

These routes may include open walkways, enclosed corridors, stairways, ramps, lifts and landings. Their form will depend on the building type, climate, level of finish and regulatory requirements.

An access bridge, threshold or other connection links the service frame to the entrance of each installed module.

Where adjacent modules are connected to form a larger internal space, the service frame may provide one or more entrances suited to the combined arrangement.

Utility infrastructure

Clear routes for pipes, cables and ducts

The service frame provides organised routes for the utilities needed by installed modules.

These may include electricity, water, drainage, communications, heating, cooling, ventilation, fire systems and other project-specific services.

Keeping shared service routes accessible can make inspection, maintenance, repair and future alteration easier than when services are concealed within inaccessible parts of the accommodation or primary structure.

Each module connects to the shared infrastructure through defined service interfaces designed for safe connection, isolation and disconnection.

Building operation

Infrastructure for everyday use and long-term change

The service frame supports both the daily operation of the building and the less frequent work required to change it.

In normal use, it provides safe circulation and access to utilities. During maintenance or module replacement, it provides working access to service connections, module entrances and the edge of the accommodation frame.

Its layout should allow work at one module to be isolated where practical, limiting disruption to neighbouring accommodation.

The service frame may also contain meters, distribution equipment, control systems, storage, refuse handling or other shared facilities needed by a particular building.

Module installation

Designed around a clear path for change

The relationship between the service frame and accommodation frame must allow accommodation modules to be installed and removed.

The arrangement may provide access from one side of the module while installation equipment approaches from another. In other designs, parts of the service frame may move, detach or temporarily open to provide the necessary installation route.

Walkways, bridges, façades, services and safety barriers must be arranged so that they do not permanently obstruct module removal.

The exact installation sequence will depend on the geometry of the building and the equipment used to handle the modules.

Relationship to the accommodation frame

Closely associated but functionally distinct

Every ExoSett accommodation frame requires a means of providing access and services to the modules it hosts. This operational role is performed by an associated service frame.

The two structures may be connected and may share some loads or stabilising systems where this is deliberately engineered, but their roles remain distinct.

The accommodation frame primarily supports the modules. The service frame primarily supports circulation, utilities and operational access.

Keeping those roles clear makes it easier to understand which parts of the building must remain when accommodation is changed.

Arrangements

Serving one frame or several

A service frame may serve a single accommodation frame positioned along one side of it.

It may also be positioned between two accommodation frames, allowing modules on both sides to share circulation, lifts, stairs and utility routes.

Larger developments may connect several service and accommodation frames into a wider circulation and infrastructure network.

The appropriate arrangement will depend on access distances, fire strategy, daylight, privacy, installation routes, structural layout and the operational needs of the building.

Variants

From an open walkway to a finished interior

A minimal service frame may be an open structure containing external stairs, walkways and exposed service routes, similar in character to access galleries serving motel rooms.

A more sophisticated service frame may be fully enclosed, insulated, heated or cooled, with lifts and finished corridors comparable to those in a conventional hotel or block of flats.

Service frames may vary in structure, width, level of enclosure, materials, fire resistance, weather protection and capacity for future services.

The ExoSett principle does not prescribe one appearance. It defines the continuing role that the service frame performs.

Distinction

More than a corridor attached to the building

The service frame is not merely decorative circulation added after the accommodation has been designed.

It is a fundamental part of the ExoSett system because it provides the continuing access and utility infrastructure that allows the accommodation modules to remain independently replaceable.

Without a practical means of reaching, disconnecting and removing a module, structural independence alone would not produce an adaptable building.

Development status

A defined role with project-specific design

The role of the service frame within ExoSett is well defined, but its detailed arrangement will vary considerably between building types and implementations.

Current development questions include structural relationships, circulation layouts, module access, fire safety, enclosure, utility distribution, service connection, installation clearances and the way one service frame may serve several accommodation frames.

Every practical service frame would require appropriate structural, architectural, accessibility, services, fire, maintenance and regulatory design.
