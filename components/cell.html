ExoSett system role

Cell

A cell is a defined space within an accommodation frame that can host one accommodation module or remain empty.

The cell identifies a continuing location and role within the building. The physical module occupying that cell may be installed, removed or replaced over time.

Definition

A place for one module

A cell is the space defined by the geometry of an accommodation frame for the installation of one accommodation module.

Each installed accommodation module occupies one cell, and each occupied cell contains one accommodation module.

A cell may also be empty. The accommodation frame must remain structurally viable whether a particular cell is occupied or not.

System role

A continuing location rather than a physical part

A cell is not itself a manufactured component. It is a defined location and role within the accommodation frame.

The frame components surrounding a cell may be repaired or replaced, and different accommodation modules may occupy the cell at different times, while the cell continues to identify the same place within the building.

This distinction between a continuing role and the replaceable parts occupying or defining it is central to the ExoSett system.

Module relationship

One occupied cell, one module

The relationship between cells and installed modules is one to one.

A module does not extend across several cells. Instead, larger accommodation is created by connecting modules installed in adjacent cells.

The distinction matters because each module remains a separately supported and replaceable unit even when it contributes to a larger combined space.

Combined spaces

Several modules can operate as one place

Modules in neighbouring cells may be connected through compatible openings to create a larger room, dwelling or operational space.

These connections may extend horizontally between modules on the same level or vertically between modules on different floors. Several connected modules could therefore form a space extending across many cells and several storeys.

The combined interior may be experienced as one place, but the individual modules remain distinct volumetric units, each occupying one cell.

Connections between modules must address structure, movement, weather protection, fire, smoke, acoustics, air leakage, thermal performance, services and safe disconnection.

Geometry

Defined by the surrounding frame

A cell is bounded and located by the frame geometry around it, including frame nodes, columns, beams and any intermediate structural elements required by the particular system.

Its usable installation envelope must account not only for the nominal dimensions of the module, but also for tolerances, clearances, supports, restraints, handling equipment, façades, services and access connections.

Cell dimensions may vary between ExoSett implementations. A hotel, dwelling, institution or other building type may use a cell geometry chosen for its own accommodation, transport and structural needs.

Within a particular frame arrangement, however, the module installed in a cell must be compatible with that cell.

Empty cells

Absence is a normal system condition

A cell does not have to contain a module at all times.

Cells may be empty during initial construction, module replacement, phased occupation, seasonal contraction, refurbishment or a longer period of reduced demand.

An empty cell must not interrupt the principal support of modules in neighbouring cells or cells above it.

The building design must address safety around an empty cell, including guarding, weather protection, fire strategy, access, temporary closure and any changes to lateral stiffness.

Support and restraint

Defined interfaces within each cell

Each cell provides defined locations at which an installed module is supported and restrained by the accommodation frame.

These interfaces transfer gravity, lateral and operational loads between the module and the frame while allowing controlled disconnection when the module is removed.

The detailed form of the supports and restraints may vary, but their positions, capacities, tolerances and installation sequence must be compatible with both the frame and the module.

A cell may also provide attachment or docking locations for installation equipment, temporary barriers, façades or other systems associated with module handling and building operation.

Access and services

Connected to the service frame

An occupied cell must allow the installed module to connect safely to the associated service frame.

This relationship provides occupant and maintenance access to the module and allows utilities to pass through defined service interfaces.

When the cell is empty, access openings, service connections and circulation edges must be safely isolated or protected.

The arrangement must also preserve a practical path for module installation and removal.

Addressing

A cell has a stable place in the frame

Cells can be identified using the coordinate system of the accommodation frame.

A cell address describes its location within the building rather than the identity of the module currently occupying it.

This allows building records, installation plans, service connections, inspections and maintenance activities to refer to a stable place even when modules are exchanged.

The detailed cell-address notation will be defined alongside the wider ExoSett frame-address system.

Distinction

The cell is not the module

The cell is a location within the accommodation frame. The module is the physical volumetric unit installed in that location.

When a module is removed, the cell does not disappear. It becomes an empty cell available for protection, inspection or occupation by another compatible module.

This separation allows the building to retain a stable structural and operational identity while its physical accommodation changes.

Development status

A defined relationship requiring detailed interfaces

The one-cell/one-module relationship is a defining rule of ExoSett.

Detailed development is still required for cell dimensions, tolerances, module supports, restraint, installation clearances, service connections, access, façades, empty-cell protection and connections between adjacent modules.

These details will vary between implementations and must be resolved through appropriate architectural, structural, services, fire, manufacturing and regulatory design.
