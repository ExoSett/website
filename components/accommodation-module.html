ExoSett component

Accommodation module

An accommodation module is a removable volumetric unit that provides a room or other usable space within an ExoSett building.

Each module is independently supported by the accommodation frame and can be installed, removed or replaced without acting as the principal support for neighbouring modules.

Definition

The usable space hosted by the frame

An accommodation module is a substantially complete volumetric unit installed within one or more cells of an accommodation frame.

It may provide a bedroom, dwelling, hotel room, workspace, treatment room, classroom or another form of enclosed and usable accommodation.

The module is distinct from the primary structural frame, the circulation system and the shared utility infrastructure of the building.

Purpose

Provide accommodation that can change independently

The principal purpose of an accommodation module is to provide the occupied or operational space within an ExoSett building.

By separating that space from the long-lived frame, access structure and shared services, the module can be manufactured, fitted out, maintained and replaced as a distinct unit.

This allows a building to respond when a room becomes damaged, outdated or unsuitable, or when a different type of accommodation is required.

Key characteristics

What defines an ExoSett accommodation module

The module is supported by the accommodation frame rather than by the accommodation module below it.

Its removal does not remove the principal structural support for neighbouring or upper modules.

It can be connected to and disconnected from the building through defined structural, access and service interfaces.

It can be handled as a distinct volumetric unit during manufacture, transport, installation, removal and relocation.

Its internal construction and fit-out can be adapted to its intended use without redefining the fundamental role of the accommodation frame.

Support and restraint

Carried by the frame and secured within the cell

An accommodation module transfers its gravity loads to defined support points in the accommodation frame.

The module must also be restrained against movement caused by wind, occupancy, vibration, handling, accidental effects and other project-specific loads.

Support and restraint arrangements must allow the module to remain secure while installed, but also permit controlled disconnection and removal when required.

A module may contribute to the stiffness or behaviour of a particular building where this is deliberately engineered. However, no individual module should become indispensable to the continuing support of surrounding accommodation.

Interfaces

Defined connections to the wider building

The module interfaces with the accommodation frame at its support, restraint and handling points.

It interfaces with the service frame through an entrance, access bridge, threshold or other circulation connection.

It connects to electricity, water, drainage, communications, ventilation and other utilities through service interfaces designed for safe connection and disconnection.

It may also include defined points for lifting, transport, positioning and engagement with installation equipment.

Manufacture and fit-out

A room that can be completed away from the building

Accommodation modules may be manufactured and substantially fitted out in a factory or workshop before delivery to the building.

Depending on their purpose, modules may arrive with internal finishes, furniture, sanitary fittings, appliances, mechanical and electrical systems, windows and external weather protection already installed.

Off-site manufacture can provide controlled working conditions, repeatable processes and the opportunity to inspect a module before it is installed.

The extent of factory completion will depend on transport limits, installation methods, module design and the requirements of the particular project.

Geometry

One cell, several cells or an intermediate arrangement

A module may occupy a single cell or extend across several cells, depending on the geometry of the accommodation frame and the needs of the building.

Module length, width and height do not have to correspond to one universal standard. Different building types may use different module sizes, and a frame may be designed to accommodate more than one module format.

Inter-nodes or other intermediate supports may be used where module dimensions do not align only with the principal frame grid.

Standard transport envelopes, including shipping-container dimensions and attachment conventions, may be useful in some implementations but do not define every possible ExoSett module.

Internal use

Different spaces within a common system

Accommodation modules can be designed for many different occupants, uses and levels of finish.

A module might contain a compact hotel room, a family dwelling, an accessible home, a clinical space, an office or a robust unit for demanding operational environments.

Modules used within the same frame need not all have identical internal layouts or commercial positions. A building may therefore support a mixture of accommodation types where the structural, dimensional, access and service interfaces are compatible.

Enclosure and weather protection

The module may be exposed or sheltered

In some ExoSett buildings, each module may form its own complete weather-resistant enclosure.

In others, modules may sit behind a shared façade, roof or outer weather shield provided by the wider building.

The appropriate arrangement will depend on climate, fire strategy, appearance, maintenance, energy performance and the intended speed and frequency of module replacement.

Whatever arrangement is chosen must preserve a safe and durable relationship between the module and the surrounding building envelope.

Replacement and relocation

Designed to leave as well as arrive

An accommodation module is not intended to become inaccessible merely because it has been installed.

The building must provide a practical route by which the module can be disconnected, handled and removed without dismantling unrelated parts of the accommodation frame.

A removed module may be refurbished, recycled, repurposed or moved to another compatible ExoSett building.

The feasibility of relocation will depend on the module’s condition, dimensions, certification, service interfaces, transport requirements and compatibility with its destination.

Variants

A shared role with many possible forms

Modules may vary in size, structure, materials, level of completion, internal layout and expected service life.

They may be built from steel, timber, composite systems or other suitable materials. They may be highly standardised or tailored to a particular building, operator or occupant group.

Some modules may be designed for frequent relocation. Others may remain in one building for many years but retain the ability to be removed when refurbishment or replacement eventually becomes necessary.

The ExoSett principle does not require every module to be identical. It requires the relationships between module, frame, access, services and installation equipment to be deliberately defined.

Distinction

Hosted accommodation rather than a structural stack

In some modular buildings, each module is part of a load-bearing stack. Modules above depend directly on the modules below.

In an ExoSett building, the accommodation frame provides the principal structural support. The module occupies space within that structure rather than serving as the essential support for the rest of the building.

This distinction makes independent module removal and replacement a defining system capability rather than an exceptional demolition operation.

Development status

A defined system role with many design questions

The role of the accommodation module within ExoSett is well defined, but no single detailed module design is prescribed for every application.

Current development questions include module dimensions, support and restraint, handling points, service interfaces, fire performance, weather protection, transport, installation tolerances and compatibility between buildings.

Every practical module would require appropriate architectural, structural, manufacturing, services, fire, acoustic, thermal, transport and regulatory design.
