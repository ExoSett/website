ExoSett component

Façade

The façade provides external enclosure, environmental protection and architectural expression for an ExoSett building.

It must perform the normal duties of a building envelope while preserving a practical route for accommodation modules to be installed, removed and replaced.

Definition

The external enclosure of the building

A façade is the external enclosure or outer face of an ExoSett building.

It may provide protection from rain, wind, heat, cold, noise and other environmental effects. It may also provide fire resistance, privacy, daylight control, security and the visible architectural character of the building.

The façade is distinct from the accommodation frame, accommodation modules and service frame, although it may connect to or interact with each of them.

Purpose

Enclose the building without preventing change

The principal purpose of the façade is to create a safe, durable and comfortable boundary between the building and its surroundings.

In an ExoSett building, the façade has an additional responsibility: it must not make accommodation-module replacement impractical.

The façade should therefore provide the required enclosure while retaining a credible way to inspect, open, remove, replace or reconfigure the parts that stand between a module and its installation route.

Key characteristics

What defines an ExoSett façade

The façade provides the environmental and architectural performance required by the building.

It preserves a practical route for module installation and removal.

Parts that obstruct a module path can be opened, moved, detached or replaced through a defined sequence.

Its connections to the accommodation frame, service frame and modules are deliberately designed and inspectable where practical.

Empty cells can be safely enclosed or protected without requiring a permanent accommodation module.

Any structural contribution made by the façade is recognised and engineered as part of the building’s load-resisting system.

Enclosure arrangements

Several ways to define the outer boundary

An ExoSett building does not require one universal façade arrangement.

An accommodation module may provide its own complete weather-resistant outer face. This may be appropriate where modules are individually sealed and the frame remains substantially open.

Alternatively, modules may sit behind a shared façade attached to the accommodation frame, service frame or another supporting structure.

A building may also use a hybrid arrangement in which module faces, shared panels, access structures and roof elements together form the complete external enclosure.

Relationship to modules

Independent, integrated or somewhere between

A façade may be independent of the accommodation modules behind it, allowing modules to be exchanged without changing the visible outer appearance of the building.

In another arrangement, the exposed face of each module may form part of the façade. Replacing the module may then also replace that part of the external elevation.

A module may also connect to shared façade components around its perimeter, including seals, flashings, fire barriers, insulation, shading and drainage details.

These interfaces must maintain environmental performance during normal use while remaining capable of controlled separation when a module is removed.

Module installation

The façade must include a route for change

A module cannot be independently replaceable if the completed façade permanently traps it within the building.

The façade may therefore contain removable panels, opening sections, demountable screens or other arrangements that expose the target cell when required.

The opening sequence must provide sufficient clearance for the module and installation equipment while protecting adjacent parts of the building.

Once the operation is complete, the façade must be capable of being reinstated and its weather, fire, acoustic, thermal and security performance verified.

Empty cells

Enclosing a cell without a module

A cell may remain empty during construction, refurbishment, replacement or a longer period of reduced demand.

The building must remain safely enclosed where the absence of a module would otherwise leave an opening in the external envelope.

Temporary or longer-term closure panels may provide weather protection, insulation, fire separation, security and visual continuity across the empty cell.

These closure arrangements must preserve the ability to reopen the cell when a module is ready to be installed.

Environmental performance

Weather, heat, sound and air

The façade must manage rainwater, wind, temperature, solar gain, moisture and air movement appropriate to the climate and use of the building.

It may include insulation, cavities, membranes, drainage paths, windows, vents, shading devices and other elements needed to achieve the intended performance.

Joints around removable panels and module interfaces are especially important because they must combine durable environmental performance with the possibility of later disassembly.

Repeated opening and reinstatement must not be assumed to preserve performance without inspection, maintenance and replacement of seals or other consumable parts.

Fire performance

Maintaining separation around replaceable elements

The façade forms part of the building’s fire strategy and must limit the spread of fire and smoke in accordance with the requirements of the particular building.

Cavities, module perimeters, panel joints, openings and connections to floors or frame components may all require fire-stopping, compartmentation or cavity barriers.

Removable elements must be capable of reinstatement without leaving hidden discontinuities in the fire-protection system.

The material selection, geometry and replacement sequence must be considered as part of the complete fire-engineered design rather than as an isolated cladding choice.

Structural behaviour

Enclosure, support and possible stiffness

The façade must support its own weight and resist wind, impact, maintenance and other relevant loads.

These loads may be transferred to the accommodation frame, service frame or a separate façade-supporting structure.

A façade system may also contribute lateral stiffness to the wider building where this is deliberately designed, analysed and detailed.

If the accommodation frame depends on that contribution, the façade becomes part of the structural system and its removal sequence, connections and temporary conditions must be engineered accordingly.

Architectural expression

The visible character of an adaptable building

The modular organisation of an ExoSett building does not require the finished façade to resemble a stack of containers or identical boxes.

A shared façade may conceal the module grid completely, express it deliberately or combine repeated elements with larger architectural gestures.

Materials, proportions, windows, balconies, shading, planting and other features can be selected for the context and intended identity of the building.

The architectural design must, however, remain compatible with inspection, maintenance, replacement and the module-installation strategy.

Change over time

A replaceable layer in its own right

Façades may have a different service life from the primary structure and the accommodation modules.

Panels, windows, insulation, finishes or shading systems may be renewed as environmental standards, appearance or building use changes.

An ExoSett façade can therefore be designed as a replaceable building layer rather than an inseparable finish applied permanently to the frame.

Replacement should preserve useful supporting infrastructure and avoid unnecessary disturbance to occupied modules where practical.

Variants

Many forms of enclosure

An ExoSett façade may use rainscreen panels, glazing, masonry, sheet materials, timber, metal, composites, fabrics, screens, planting or other suitable systems.

It may be lightweight or substantial, open or sealed, transparent or opaque, flat or articulated.

Some façades may be assembled as cell-sized removable panels. Others may use larger sections serving several cells, provided those sections can be managed safely when access is required.

The façade may also incorporate balconies, shading, maintenance access, solar panels, signage or other building-specific features.

Distinction

Not the same as an empty-cell closure

The façade is the wider external enclosure and architectural face of the building.

An empty-cell closure is a panel, frame or temporary assembly used specifically to protect a cell that does not contain a module.

An empty-cell closure may form part of the visible façade, but it performs a more limited and location-specific role.

A stabilising closure frame may also provide guarding or stiffness, but any such structural role must be distinguished from enclosure alone.

Development status

A necessary system layer with many possible implementations

The role of the façade within ExoSett is defined, but no single façade system is prescribed for every building.

Development questions include attachment, module clearances, removable sections, empty-cell closures, fire barriers, weathering, drainage, thermal continuity, acoustic performance, maintenance, replacement and possible contribution to structural stiffness.

Façade design must also be coordinated closely with module installation equipment, the service frame, roofs and the operational requirements of occupied buildings.

Every practical façade would require project-specific architectural, structural, fire, environmental, access, maintenance and regulatory design.
