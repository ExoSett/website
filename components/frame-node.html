Frame node

A frame node is a structural component located at an intersection in the accommodation-frame geometry.

It connects columns, beams and other supported elements, receives loads from the surrounding frame and modules, and transfers those loads through defined structural paths.

Definition

The structural junction of the frame

A frame node is the component through which principal structural members meet at a defined location in an ExoSett accommodation frame.

Depending on its position, a node may connect vertical columns, horizontal beams, module supports, lashings, bracing, façades, installation equipment or other parts of the building.

The frame node is therefore more than a simple connector. It is a controlled structural interface through which loads, alignment and geometry are maintained.

Purpose

Join the frame and preserve its load paths

The principal purpose of a frame node is to connect structural members while transferring loads safely between them.

It helps establish the regular geometry of the accommodation frame, provides repeatable connection locations and allows individual frame members to be assembled, inspected, removed or replaced.

Because loads from several directions may meet at a node, its strength, stiffness, detailing and connection behaviour are critical to the performance of the wider frame.

Key characteristics

What defines an ExoSett frame node

A frame node occupies a defined address within the accommodation frame.

It provides repeatable interfaces for the structural members and other components connected at that address.

It transfers loads between those connected elements through deliberately designed load paths.

It helps maintain the alignment, dimensions and tolerances of the surrounding frame geometry.

It may be removed and replaced without changing the continuing frame address or structural role associated with its location.

Its connection behaviour must be understood as part of the structural analysis of the whole accommodation frame.

Load paths

Where loads meet and move through the frame

Loads from modules, beams, roofs, façades, lashings and other supported elements may be introduced into the frame at or near a frame node.

Gravity loads must pass through the node into the column or support below. Horizontal and torsional effects may pass between beams, columns, bracing and adjacent parts of the frame.

The internal structure of the node must therefore provide direct and sufficiently robust paths between its connection interfaces.

Features that appear small in the overall building may carry concentrated forces. Their dimensions, material, continuity and attachment to the rest of the node must be treated as structural matters rather than incidental details.

Connections

Receiving structural members from several directions

A frame node may provide sockets, sleeves, plates, bearings, fasteners or other interfaces for receiving connected components.

Vertical interfaces connect columns above and below. Horizontal interfaces connect beams that extend across the width or depth of the frame.

Additional attachment points may support modules, lashings, temporary bracing, façades, roofs, installation equipment or maintenance systems.

These interfaces must allow components to be assembled accurately and secured firmly while still supporting inspection, maintenance and controlled disassembly.

Connection behaviour

Pinned, semi-rigid or rigid

Structural analysis must represent how the node and its connected members behave under load.

A connection may act approximately as pinned, allowing significant rotation; as rigid, transferring bending moments; or as semi-rigid, with behaviour between those two idealisations.

The appropriate representation depends on the detailed geometry, material, tolerances, fasteners, contact conditions and deformation of the node and connected members.

This behaviour affects the stiffness, stability, force distribution and bracing requirements of the entire accommodation frame.

Geometry

A repeatable junction within a three-dimensional frame

Frame nodes are positioned at defined intersections in the three-dimensional geometry of the accommodation frame.

A typical node may connect members extending in the width, depth and height directions of the frame. Boundary nodes may omit connections that would otherwise extend beyond the outer edge of the structure.

Different node variants may therefore be required at corners, edges, internal intersections, foundations, roofs or intermediate structural locations.

Wherever practical, ExoSett seeks to limit unnecessary variation so that node manufacture, assembly, replacement and reuse remain manageable.

Frame address

The location remains when the part changes

Every frame node occupies a defined location within the coordinate system of the accommodation frame.

A notation such as n(x,y,z) identifies the frame address of a node rather than the individual manufactured component installed there.

If a damaged node is removed and replaced, the physical part changes but the frame address and structural role remain the same.

This distinction supports clear design records, inspection, maintenance, assembly instructions and component traceability.

Module support

Supporting accommodation at defined locations

Frame nodes may provide or support the bearing and restraint interfaces used by accommodation modules.

These interfaces establish where module loads enter the accommodation frame and help position the module accurately within its cell.

The arrangement must support the installed module securely while preserving a practical means of release during module removal.

Module-support details must also account for tolerances, movement, vibration, durability, drainage and the repeated installation or removal cycles anticipated for the building.

Frame stability

A connection point for stiffness and bracing

Frame nodes influence the stiffness of the accommodation frame through the behaviour of their beam and column connections.

They may also provide attachment points for lashings, diagonal braces, stabilising frames or other systems used to resist racking and twisting.

The forces created by those systems may be substantial and may act in directions different from the principal gravity-load path.

Bracing attachments and the surrounding node material must therefore be designed as part of the overall structural system.

Assembly and replacement

A component intended to be installed accurately

Frame nodes provide repeatable junctions that can help simplify the assembly of columns, beams and other frame components.

Their geometry must support accurate positioning and alignment while accommodating the tolerances associated with manufacture, foundations, site assembly and thermal movement.

Connections should be inspectable and maintainable where practical. A node should also be capable of replacement if it becomes damaged or no longer satisfies the requirements of the building.

Replacement may require temporary support and a carefully engineered sequence because the node forms part of the active structural load path.

Variants

Different locations, related structural roles

A standard frame node may connect the principal members surrounding the corner of one or more cells.

An inter-node performs a related role at an intermediate location, providing additional connections or support between principal frame positions.

Foundation nodes, roof nodes, edge nodes and corner nodes may require different interfaces because not every structural direction continues through them.

Nodes may also vary according to material, fire protection, corrosion exposure, manufacturing process, connection method and the loads associated with a particular implementation.

Manufacture

Complex geometry requiring controlled production

A frame node may combine several load-bearing interfaces within a compact three-dimensional component.

Possible manufacturing approaches include casting, fabrication from plates and sections, machining, forging or combinations of processes.

The chosen process must deliver the required strength, dimensional accuracy, surface condition, inspectability and production consistency.

Manufacturing design must also consider cost, repairability, component identification, quality assurance and the possibility of reuse.

Distinction

More than a bracket between members

A frame node does not merely hold several structural members in approximately the right place.

It establishes geometry, transfers concentrated loads, influences connection stiffness and provides interfaces for other systems associated with the cell.

Its internal structure and its connection to surrounding members are therefore central to the safety and performance of the accommodation frame.

Development status

A critical component under detailed engineering development

The role of the frame node within ExoSett is well defined, and detailed geometric modelling is under way.

Current development work includes member interfaces, internal load paths, module-support locations, connection stiffness, bracing attachments, tolerances, drainage, manufacture, assembly and replacement.

Independent structural engineering work is examining the behaviour of the wider frame and the implications of pinned, semi-rigid and rigid connection assumptions.

Any practical frame node would require detailed analysis, material specification, manufacturing development, inspection, testing and project-specific regulatory approval.
